# REFLECTION6.md — Challenges in Agile Planning
## Campus Lost & Found System (CLAFS)

---

Agile methodology is built on the assumption of a team — a product owner to prioritise, a Scrum master to facilitate, developers to estimate, and stakeholders to provide feedback. Working through this assignment as a solo developer forced me to occupy all of these roles simultaneously, and the internal tension that created was surprisingly revealing.

**The first challenge was story estimation.** In a real Scrum team, story points are assigned through planning poker — a collaborative process where different team members bring different perspectives on complexity, and the discussion itself surfaces hidden assumptions. Alone, I had no one to challenge my estimates. I found myself consistently underestimating stories I was comfortable with and overestimating those I had less experience with. For example, I initially estimated the Cloudinary image upload integration at 2 story points, but when I broke it down into actual tasks — Multer configuration, Cloudinary SDK setup, file validation, the React upload component, and error handling — it became clear it was at least a 3. The act of writing the task breakdown was what corrected my estimate, which made me realise that estimation without task decomposition is largely guesswork.

**The second challenge was MoSCoW prioritization.** On the surface, prioritising the backlog seemed straightforward — authentication first, then reporting, then search, and so on. But the moment I had to justify each prioritisation decision in writing, I noticed I was rationalising choices I had already made intuitively rather than reasoning from stakeholder evidence. For instance, I initially marked notifications as Must-have because they felt important. When I reviewed the Assignment 4 stakeholder analysis more carefully, I realised that while notifications improve the experience, the core workflow — report, search, claim, approve — functions without them. Downgrading notifications to Should-have was the correct decision, but it required me to argue against my own initial instinct, which is uncomfortable when you are both the product owner and the developer.

**The third challenge was sprint scope.** I selected 5 user stories totalling 16 story points for Sprint 1. In a real team with a known velocity, this would be validated against historical data. As a solo developer with no prior velocity measurement, I had to make a judgment call about what is achievable in two weeks alongside academic obligations. I felt the pull to include more — to also add search functionality or the report found item story — because they felt logically connected. But I resisted, applying the principle that a sprint goal should be focused and deliverable. Overcommitting in Sprint 1 and failing to complete stories would create technical debt and erode confidence in the planning process, even if the only person experiencing that erosion is myself.

**The fourth challenge was writing user stories that were truly independent.** The INVEST criteria require that stories be independent of each other, but in a sequential system like CLAFS, almost every story depends on a previous one. Registration must exist before login. Login must exist before item reporting. I resolved this by treating dependencies as scheduling constraints rather than violations of independence — each story is independently testable and independently valuable, even if it cannot be delivered before its dependency is resolved.

The deeper lesson from this assignment is that Agile is fundamentally a social methodology. Its ceremonies — standups, retrospectives, planning poker — exist to create shared understanding across a team. Doing it alone removes that social layer and forces all of the difficult conversations inward. The resistance I felt was real, and working through it made me appreciate why these practices exist.
